# After-Action Report: PACT July 26

**Course dates:** July 13–17, 2026  
**Report date:** July 17, 2026  
**Cohort:** 37 active learners in 4 squads  
**Evidence used:** PACT grades, assignment/submission activity, and anonymous post-course survey aggregates

## Executive summary

The cohort demonstrated strong measured performance and broad participation. Among the 31 learners with matched pretest and posttest records, the average normalized score rose from 83.4% to 99.1%, a gain of 15.6 percentage points. All 31 matched learners improved. Individual coursework, excluding the intentionally invalidated Drop 7 scores, averaged 94.2% across the 34 learners with recorded scores.

Participation was also high: 36 of 37 learners (97.3%) had at least one assignment submission, and 32 learners completed the anonymous post-course survey (86.5% response rate).

The principal issue was not course relevance or instructional clarity. Survey respondents strongly endorsed real-world applicability, instructor explanations, and the value of collaboration. The clearest weaknesses were the pace and volume of evidence drops, insufficient independent investigation time, and application reliability. These issues should be the priority for the next delivery.

## What happened

PACT July 26 ran as a five-day, squad-based Packet Heist course. The platform released 78 assignment records to the cohort: 68 challenges, 7 modules, 2 assessments, and 1 survey. Some releases were role- or squad-specific, so the release count is an inventory count rather than 78 assignments expected from every learner.

Drop 7 grades were deliberately invalidated after the course activity. They remain outside the score analysis in this report so that the invalidated event does not affect learner or cohort performance.

### Anonymous survey results

The post-course survey received 32 responses, exceeding the minimum anonymous reporting threshold of 3. Results below are aggregate-only; no names or response-level comments are included.

| Survey category | Mean rating (5-point items) | Positive responses |
|---|---:|---:|
| Instruction & Support | 4.09 | 84.4% |
| Course Content | 3.92 | 75.4% |
| Logistics | 3.88 | 69.5% |
| Evidence Drop & Investigation Format | 3.39 | 49.4% |
| Application Experience | 3.34 | 46.1% |

Strongest individual findings:

- 93.8% agreed or strongly agreed that they could apply the learning to real-world security work.
- 90.6% agreed or strongly agreed that instructor explanations were clear and actionable.
- 81.3% agreed or strongly agreed that collaboration helped reveal connections, challenge assumptions, or improve conclusions.

Most significant friction:

- Only 18.8% agreed that evidence drops arrived at an appropriate pace and contained a manageable amount of information.
- Only 25.0% agreed that they had enough independent investigation time before collaboration.
- Only 21.9% rated the application as reliable during class exercises.
- Only 37.5% agreed that the overall course pacing was appropriate.

When asked for the single change that would most improve the format, the most common selections were smaller, more frequent evidence drops (9), another change described in free text (7), more squad/class collaboration time (6), and more independent investigation time (4). Only 1 respondent selected keeping the format unchanged.

Twenty-two respondents provided an optional recommendation. Aggregate theme detection found 14 mentions related to investigation time, 10 related to collaboration, 8 related to application/tools, and 5 related to evidence pacing or volume. A comment can contribute to more than one theme.

## Analysis

### Sustains

1. **Keep the applied investigation model.** Learners saw clear real-world relevance, and collaboration was one of the highest-rated elements of the evidence-drop format.
2. **Preserve instructor-led explanation and support.** Instruction was the strongest survey category and should remain the anchor for complex transitions.
3. **Continue normalized pre/post measurement.** It provides a fair comparison when assessment maxima differ and showed broad improvement in the matched group.
4. **Retain squad work with explicit individual checkpoints.** Squad challenge outcomes were strong, but individual checkpoints are needed to distinguish shared completion from individual mastery.

### Improves

1. **Reduce evidence-load spikes.** Break large drops into smaller staged releases with a clear “must analyze now” subset and optional enrichment evidence.
2. **Protect independent analysis time.** Add a timed individual hypothesis step before squad discussion so learners can form and record an initial conclusion.
3. **Stabilize the application before the next cohort.** Treat reliability and slow workflows as course-delivery risks; complete a full instructor/learner rehearsal using the production path.
4. **Clarify challenge completion state.** Reconcile shared and role-specific challenge status so completed work does not remain visibly in progress.
5. **Close assessment coverage gaps.** Investigate the 5 learners without paired assessments and the 3 learners without valid individual coursework scores while records are still operationally fresh.

## Methodology and limitations

- Data was extracted from the production PACT records on July 17, 2026.
- The AAR contains aggregate cohort data only. Survey results are anonymous and are not joined to learner identities.
- Pre/post improvement uses matched learners and normalized percent-correct scores; it is descriptive and does not establish causation or statistical significance.
- Individual coursework summaries exclude Drop 7 and exclude squad-graded work.
- Mean assignment percentages treat each recorded assignment grade equally rather than weighting by maximum points.
- Submission activity is a platform participation proxy. It does not establish physical attendance, time on task, or individual contribution to shared squad work.
- Survey theme counts are keyword-assisted aggregates and may overlap; they are directional, not a substitute for qualitative coding.
